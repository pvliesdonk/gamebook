## Children's & YA Conventions · Tabletop

**Rules complexity is collapsed to a single resolution gesture.** Kids'-RPG lines push the
entire ruleset onto one page or one mechanic. Amazing Tales' play rules "fit on one page"
of a 96-page book — the other 95 pages are GM-facing guidance, not systems — and are "so
simple a four year old will be able to explain them back to you." [researched][1] No Thank
You, Evil! uses a single d6-vs-target-number roll, with tokens spent to bump the result.
[researched][2] The convention: one die, one comparison, optional spend — no stacked
subsystems.

**Resolution is simplified, not merely abbreviated.** Common kid-RPG adaptations swap the
d20 for a smaller die matched to a child's counting range ("if your child is only able to
count to 10... swap the d20 for a d10"), replace numeric rolls with pass/fail tokens drawn
from a cup, and fix damage at flat values ("You deal 5 points! That's half the health!")
rather than rolling pools. [researched][3] Bookkeeping is deliberately shrunk: spell lists
capped at 3–4 options, inventory at 2–3 items, and health pools scaled down by an order of
magnitude (70 HP → 7 HP with proportional damage). [researched][3]

**Reading load is offloaded to images and to the adult.** Character definition is kept to a
single short line "most players should be able to at least kind of read or write,"
[researched][2] stats are shown pictorially (a flexing arm for strength), text blocks are
moved off the main sheet, and abilities are printed as picture-cards a non-reader can hold
up. [researched][3] Text-as-cognitive-load is the constraint being engineered around, which
is why the *rules text* lives with the GM/parent while the *player surface* stays near-wordless.

**Session length tracks attention span, not story arc.** Amazing Tales runs "between 20
minutes and an hour... about the time it takes to read a bedtime story." [researched][1] No
Thank You, Evil! is roughly two hours to learn-and-play and supports both one-shots and
campaigns. [researched][2] The short-session default is a design parameter, not a limitation.

**Content boundaries and "safe stakes" are mechanized, not left to GM tact.** No Thank You,
Evil! gives every player a standing veto — anyone may shout "No thank you, Evil!" to instantly
end an encounter they dislike — plus a table token that signals the Guide to steer away from a
topic. [researched][4] Its "Awesome" stat lets players spend tokens to *help* each other with
no penalty to the giver, normalizing cooperation over competition. [researched][2] Adaptation
guides reinforce low-stakes framing: start with "just tell a story" (no dice), introduce
randomization only once roleplay is comfortable, and avoid death/grim consequence as a default
outcome. [researched][3] The therapeutic literature frames this as the value proposition —
TTRPGs are "a low-stakes environment where individuals can 'fail' with minimal social
consequences." [researched][5] The "Absent Adult" / safe-stakes framing (peril is real to the
character but the *table* is guaranteed safe; the child-hero is competent and the threat is
always escapable) is the tabletop instantiation of children's-fiction conventions — here
enforced by an explicit safety mechanism rather than by authorial restraint. [synthesis]

## Audience & Age-Targeting · Tabletop

**Two distinct "ages" sit on a tabletop box, and only one is about safety.** A legal/safety
mark (CE, choking-hazard "3+") protects toddlers from small parts; the prominent box
"recommended age" is a *publisher-set complexity grade*, "based on complexity, knowledge or
skill" rather than safety. [researched][6] This is why a mechanically simple game can carry a
14+ flag purely because its card text is "slightly too wordy." [researched][6] There is no
ESRB-equivalent board: "no hard and fast rule for age ratings... unlike movies or video games
which have official rating boards," so publishers set the grade by comparison to existing
market titles and their read of developmental milestones. [researched][7] The grade is defined
as the minimum age that "could play and enjoy the game without assistance." [researched][7]

**The age-banding levers are reading, handling, patience, and theme.** The factors publishers
weigh are explicit: reading comprehension (card/manual text), component handling (physical
dexterity), length/patience versus attention span, and thematic content. [researched][6][7]
"Young kids can't be expected to read every card" and "can't be expected to hold a hand of
cards" — so reading load and grip ergonomics directly raise or lower the printed age.
[researched][7]

**Component ergonomics for small hands is a first-class design constraint.** The toddler/preschool
band favors oversized wooden pieces (the genre exemplar being First Orchard's large bits "perfect
for little hands"), avoids fan-of-cards holding, and eliminates choking-hazard small parts.
[researched][8] Cooperative or no-loser structures and 5–10 minute play times are the norm at
the youngest band. [researched][8] Graduated/tiered lines (e.g. Family Fantasy RPG starting at
~age 3 in tier 1, adding one spell or item per step) let the *same* property scale up reading
and tracking load as the child grows. [researched][3]

**How this differs from IF reading-level/session-length bands.** [synthesis] In interactive
fiction, reading level *is* the gameplay surface — vocabulary and sentence complexity gate the
experience directly, and "session length" is reader-paced and effectively unbounded (you can
stop mid-passage). On a tabletop, reading is something designers *route around*: the canonical
moves are pushing rules text to a parent/GM, replacing words with pictograms and tokens, and
capping the live reading a child must do to a single card or line. Tabletop session length is a
hard *design parameter* tuned to attention span and group patience (5–10 min at preschool, 20–60
min for early kids' RPGs), not a reader-controlled variable. And tabletop carries a second axis
IF lacks entirely — physical component ergonomics (grip, choke-safety, piece size) — which can
dominate the printed age band independent of any text. The shared spine across both media is
graduated bands keyed to developmental stage; the divergence is that IF targets the band through
*language*, while tabletop targets it through *offloaded text + physical handling + bounded time*.
