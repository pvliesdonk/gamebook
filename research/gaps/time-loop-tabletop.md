## Time Loop Patterns · Tabletop

**The form exists as a published, distinct design tradition, not just a campaign
houserule.** itch.io carries a sizable `time-loop` physical-games tag with dozens
of shipped titles, several of them GMless and structured entirely around diegetic
reset [1]. Concrete published examples span multiple structures:

- *Time To Drop* (Marn S) — GMless heist game where a crew relives the same 24
  hours to perfect a final job; uses dice plus a tarot/oracle deck for
  complications, and notably NPCs "retain impressions across loops" so the reset
  is not total [1][2]. [researched]
- *Lo/oper* (nattwentea) — GMless storytelling game, 1–4 players, about breaking
  free of a loop by answering card-drawn prompts [3]. [researched]
- *RESET* (Alfredo Tarancón) — team game preventing a catastrophe across a
  *limited* number of cycles before player-characters suffer mental breakdown;
  the loop budget is itself the pressure mechanic [2]. [researched]
- *ON REPEAT*, *Too O'Clock*, *Thunder In Our Hearts* — two-player loop games;
  *Too O'Clock* tracks progression by coin flips across "seven distinct iterations
  showing increasing loop divergence," i.e. iterations are explicitly numbered and
  meant to diverge rather than repeat verbatim [1][2]. [researched]
- *Dawn of the First Day* (Riley Hopkins) — loop game inspired by Poker, *Fiasco*,
  and *Majora's Mask*; the deck-of-cards economy is its loop clock [4]. [researched]
- *No Future* (GMless punk-horror), *Grim Repeater* (gothic die-trying loop), and
  *TCA: Loop Breaker* (a 36-word brochure game foregrounding the exit condition)
  show the pattern compresses down to micro-game scale [1]. [researched]

**The craft conversation is real and converges on four problems distinct from the
interactive-fiction treatment.** Where IF can store loop-state and gate progress in
code, the tabletop GM must hand-track it, so the published advice is procedural:

1. **Exit conditions must be stated up front, not discovered.** The dominant
   guidance is that "it's vital to consider how the characters get out of the time
   loop" and to make "very clear from the get-go what [the] character must do to
   get out" — typically something concrete like keeping a creature alive or denying
   an antagonist a MacGuffin [5]. The reset *trigger* is a separate design choice
   (loop on sleep, on death, or on a fixed clock), each producing different play —
   e.g. "it resets every time the party sleeps, forcing them to stay awake… while
   risking exhaustion" [6]. [researched]

2. **Player meta-knowledge is the deliberate engine, and it is the progress
   metric.** Across sources the loop is framed so "the time traveler keeps their
   knowledge and is aware of what transpired," and because brute force is erased by
   the reset, *accumulated understanding across iterations is the only thing that
   advances* [7]. Designers are told to treat the scenario "as a giant puzzle…
   [where] party knowledge is the progress metric," sometimes withholding mechanical
   XP/experience until the mystery is solved while letting investigative knowledge
   carry over [5][7]. This is the cell's defining inversion: player table-knowledge
   is *licensed* as character knowledge by fiat of the fiction. [researched]

3. **Anti-tedium pacing is the central failure mode and gets explicit
   counter-tooling.** Sources warn loops are "frustrating to deal with" and must be
   cleared with players first [3]. The recurring fixes: a "fast-forward" convention
   — "allow your players to fast-forward through sections they have already
   completed or survived," skipping redundant checks but pausing where a prior
   attempt *failed* so adaptation matters [6]; periodic **anomalies** (off-schedule
   NPC behaviour, unexpected creatures) that both break monotony and double as loop
   hints [6]; and a hard warning to **bound the loop budget per session** —
   "letting the players restart the loop more than once per session requires way
   too much preparation," so most advice caps resets at roughly one per session [7].
   A complementary contingency: prep for the group solving it *too fast* (within
   15 minutes of a 2-hour slot), shifting the back half to fixing the loop's root
   cause [5]. [researched]

4. **Tracking what changes between loops is a GM bookkeeping discipline, and it is
   loop-bounded prep.** The concrete instruction is to "create a timeline" of "NPC
   schedules, major events, when routes are closed, and objects lost or stolen,"
   on the expectation that "players will naturally replicate this tracking" [6].
   Two consistency techniques recur: pre-writing fixed NPC dialogue so a repeated
   loop reads identically [7], and a "Chekhov's Gun" rule — once you place an
   out-of-place item, "that thing now constantly needs to be there" at the same
   beat each loop, so the world stays diffable [7]. To restore stakes that the
   reset otherwise erases, designers re-introduce **permanence**: loop-aware NPCs
   who remember and form opinions, or items designated cursed/blessed that
   permanently leave the reset cycle, and **rival parties** who are also loop-aware
   to counterbalance player omniscience [5][6]. [researched]

**Synthesis — how the tabletop cell differs from IF.** The IF time-loop offloads
state-tracking, gating, and "what changed" diffing to the engine; the player's
meta-knowledge is laundered through save/restart and parser memory. Tabletop has no
engine, so every one of those concerns becomes an explicit human procedure: a stated
exit condition (vs. coded win-gate), a GM-maintained loop timeline (vs. world-state
variables), a social fast-forward convention (vs. instant restart), and a per-session
loop budget driven by prep cost rather than compute. The defining shared move —
player knowledge persisting while world-state rewinds — is identical, but tabletop
*surfaces* it as a table contract (and even weaponises it via loop-aware rivals/NPCs),
whereas IF buries it in the simulation. The published-game spread (GMless micro-games
through D&D campaign frames) shows the pattern is mature enough to have sub-genres,
which supports treating this as a genuine, occupied cell rather than a thin one. [synthesis]
