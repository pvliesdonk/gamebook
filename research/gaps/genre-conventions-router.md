The seven IF genre-conventions notes share one underlying shape: each genre is a **contract** the
work makes with its audience, served by **one dominant craft lever** that, if mishandled, breaks
the contract. The router below names the contract, the lever, and how the contract travels into the
other media. Cross-media adaptation columns lean on each note's own See-Also cross-references; the
broader cross-media matrix marks most narrative genres ◐ (partial, contract carries but tooling
differs) in Live and Tabletop and – (absent / not a meaningful axis) in Puzzle-Hunt, with mystery
and horror the conspicuous exceptions that carry strongly into live play. [synthesis]

## Per-genre notes

### Mystery & Detective
The cardinal rule is that all puzzle pieces are present to the reader; you may misdirect attention
but never withhold information, and the climax must land as "surprising AND inevitable." [corpus:
mystery] In IF this becomes a routing problem — branching can hide necessary clues, so crucial
evidence must be reachable by multiple paths, with a notebook/recap and the option to be *wrong* as
a meaningful failure state. [corpus: mystery] The contract travels almost unchanged into live play:
murder-mystery party and jubensha inherit the same fair-play tradition with evidence distributed
across players and acts. [corpus: mystery See-Also]

### Horror
Horror works by making the reader fear *for* a character they care about, then balancing sustained
dread against sparing shock — "the rule of less," where the unseen outscares the revealed. [corpus:
horror] The defining IF mechanic is the felt-vulnerability layer: sanity, corruption, resource
scarcity, and real (but not cheap) death states, with agency tuned to a sweet spot that never
guarantees safety. [corpus: horror] It is the genre that ports most naturally to physical space —
escape-room confinement and theme-and-immersion run on the same isolation-wrongness-decay engine,
making horror a strong ◐/carry into Live. [corpus: horror See-Also; synthesis]

### Fantasy
The genre's load-bearing decision is Sanderson's First Law: magic can only solve problems in
proportion to how well the reader understands it, so the craft lever is establishing rules, limits,
and costs before magic does any narrative work. [corpus: fantasy] Soft magic buys wonder but must
not resolve plot; hard magic enables puzzles and strategy — most IF mixes them (player powers hard,
ambient forces soft). [corpus: fantasy] In tabletop the rules externalize cleanly into a
system-vs-setting split, which is why fantasy's cross-media carry is strongest in TTRPG-adjacent
forms. [synthesis; corpus: fantasy See-Also]

### Science Fiction
SF asks the reader to accept one big lie (the novum) and then honors it by following the ripple
effects rigorously; the hard/soft subgenre choice sets reader expectations the way the magic
contract does in fantasy. [corpus: sci-fi] Its distinctive IF lever is the **diegetic interface** —
the text *is* the terminal; logs, glitches, and AI companions deliver exposition and hints inside
the fiction rather than around it, provided technobabble stays flavor and never becomes a
problem-solving wildcard. [corpus: sci-fi] The novum's rules carry into tabletop via the same
system-vs-setting and format-taxonomy scaffolding fantasy uses. [synthesis; corpus: sci-fi See-Also]

### Historical Fiction
The promise is "small lies, big truth" — telescope a meeting from Tuesday to Thursday if drama
needs it, but never let the wrong side win the famous battle. [corpus: historical] The dominant
craft lever is the research-to-display ratio (know ten times more than you show) coupled with
four-axis anachronism control: vocabulary, technology, concept, and social register. [corpus:
historical] IF adds a player-choice-versus-fixed-history tension resolved by separating macro
history (fixed) from micro history (the player's personal, flexible thread); the same period-setting
discipline and source posture govern its tabletop and live adaptations. [corpus: historical;
synthesis]

### Children's & YA
The cardinal rule is to write at full quality and never moralize — in IF a choice with a visibly
"correct" answer is not a choice but a quiz, so meaning must emerge from consequence. [corpus:
children/YA] The organizing craft distinction is the two orientations (middle-grade discovers self
in relation to home; YA leaves home to discover self in relation to the world), with the Absent
Adult archetype existing precisely to clear narrative space for player agency. [corpus: children/YA]
This genre owns the gamebook lineage directly — second-person address and the Skill/Stamina/Luck
stat layer are its tabletop ancestors — and its age-band content gates (ESRB/PEGI/COPPA) carry
across every medium. [corpus: children/YA; synthesis]

### Tropes & Archetypes
Tropes are not the enemy; they resonate because they encode an emotional contract, and the only
honest subversion is one that understands *why* a trope works and then fulfills that truth freshly
(inversion, deconstruction, hybridization, perspective shift). [corpus: tropes; corpus: fantasy]
This note is the cross-genre connective tissue rather than a genre proper — its Cross-Genre Migration
table shows the same role (Mentor, Betrayer, Trickster, Threshold Guardian) re-skinning across
fantasy, SF, mystery, horror, romance, and historical settings. [corpus: tropes] That migration *is*
the cross-media lever: in IF, player choice can move a character between archetype roles
dynamically, so archetypes carry into every medium that has characters at all. [corpus: tropes;
synthesis]
