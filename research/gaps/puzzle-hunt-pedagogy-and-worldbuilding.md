## Teaching Through Play · Puzzle-Hunt

A hunt teaches its own solving conventions almost entirely *in-band* — the puzzles
themselves are the curriculum, not a rulebook — and the genre leans on a small set of
repeated literacies that newcomers must internalise.

- **No declared instructions; identification is step one.** A defining convention is
  that "you cannot count on a puzzle hunt puzzle to declare itself at face value" —
  figuring out *what the puzzle is* is itself part of the puzzle. [researched][2] The
  onramp advice is pattern-recognition: "If there's a list of clues, you should
  probably try solving them. If there are a bunch of pictures, you should probably try
  identifying them," while warning that hunts deliberately subvert these expectations
  through misdirection. [researched][1]

- **The four-beat puzzle shape is the core literacy taught.** Beginner guides frame
  every puzzle as: flavor text → identify what it is → solve it → *extract* a final
  answer. [researched][2] **Extraction** — pushing a solved puzzle "a step further" to
  pull out a final word or phrase — is the skill that separates hunts from ordinary
  puzzles and is the hardest thing for first-timers to learn. [researched][1][2]

- **Indexing is the single most-taught extraction primitive.** New solvers are drilled
  on indexing (taking the Nth letter of a word/phrase, skipping spaces and
  punctuation) as "perhaps the most common way numbers are used in puzzles," and on the
  reflex habit "every time you open a puzzle, you should check if the first letters
  spell something." Related primitives include overlapping (the shared Nth letter of a
  word pair). [researched][1]

- **Flavor text is the sanctioned hint channel.** Beginners are taught to read flavor
  text for oblique encoding hints — "mentions of telegrams or transmissions may be a
  hint for Morse; any mention of blindness or feeling for things may be a hint for
  Braille" — so the prose that looks decorative is actually the onramp's teaching
  surface. [researched][1]

- **ROT13 functions as a community spoiler/hint norm, not just a cipher.** Because
  ROT13 is its own inverse (encrypt = decrypt), it is "often used online to hide
  spoilers when better formatting is not available," letting solvers share graduated
  hints without spoiling. [researched][1] (Beginner guides tend to treat it as a
  technical reference rather than explicitly codifying the social norm. [synthesis])

- **Designed beginner onramps exist as named events.** Onboarding is deliberately
  scaffolded by gentle, archived events — Puzzled Pint is repeatedly recommended as the
  first hunt for complete newcomers (free, worldwide, archived), with curated
  "easiest non-trivial" starter puzzles elsewhere. [researched][1][2]

- **A meta-literacy of record-keeping is taught early.** Solvers are told to save every
  answer because the **meta** ("boss battle") consumes prior solutions, and collaborative
  Google Sheets are the de-facto standard workspace. Failing to save work "will render
  the meta puzzle unsolvable." [researched][1][2]

**Synthesis for the guide:** a hunt's pedagogy is *progressive disclosure through
difficulty curve* — early puzzles are easy *and* expose one mechanic cleanly so the
solver builds the extraction/indexing/flavor-reading vocabulary they'll need when later
puzzles combine and disguise those same moves. The teaching is implicit by design; the
"how to solve" guides (vero.site, Room Escape Artist, alexirpan) exist precisely because
that implicit curriculum has a steep first step. [synthesis]

## Worldbuilding Patterns · Puzzle-Hunt

For the ARG/puzzle-hunt-with-narrative end of the medium, worldbuilding consistency is
not flavor — it is the load-bearing illusion, governed by the **TINAG** ("This Is Not A
Game") philosophy.

- **TINAG sets the consistency bar.** Elan Lee's three rules (GDC 2002): don't tell
  anyone, don't define the game space, don't build a game — instead "build experiences
  that are games that don't know that they are games." [researched][3][4] The
  consequence is a total-fidelity world: "any phone number or email address mentioned
  works, and any website acknowledged exists." [researched][4]

- **Voice consistency across scattered artefacts is the failure point named in
  practice.** Each site and character "has to have an appropriate and distinctive
  voice"; the canonical break case is when the illusion shatters because, e.g.,
  identical voices answer all of a fictional company's phones — at which point "the
  illusion would be broken." [researched][3] This is the artefact-level corollary of
  canon drift: continuity is maintained per-artefact (aesthetics, voice, working
  contact details), not just in a central story bible. [synthesis]

- **"The curtain" is the structural mechanism for consistency.** Borrowed from "pay no
  attention to the man behind the curtain," the curtain is the separation between
  puppetmasters and players: puppetmasters never communicate as themselves, only
  *through* characters and game design. [researched][4] This is what lets canon stay
  consistent — every authored utterance is in-world, so there is no out-of-world voice
  to contradict it. The "curtain problem" is keeping that wall intact across a long,
  multi-channel campaign. [synthesis]

- **Canon is co-authored, which is the main source of drift.** Puppetmasters control
  most of the story but incorporate player content and "intentionally leave 'white
  space' for the players to fill in," adapting the narrative in response to player
  analysis and speculation monitored via online discussion. [researched][4][5] The
  story "continues in the present, shaped by the designers in response to the players'
  discoveries and beliefs." [researched][5]

- **Real-time adaptation forces retcons; timeline length is the stressor.** Because
  puppetmasters "alter the game's content in real time, enhancing certain aspects,
  editing out others," long campaigns accumulate continuity pressure — and players
  "far outnumber the puppetmasters," so their collective scrutiny surfaces every
  inconsistency. [researched][5] A documented stressor is *unexpected timeline drift*:
  Funcom's *The Secret World* gate, planted pre-2009, was not discovered until 2013,
  forcing the puppetmasters to retcon/adapt the scenario to a setting four years later
  than authored. [researched][5]

- **The control/influence balance is the governing tension.** There is "a delicate
  balance between the influence of the Players and the control of the Puppetmasters"
  that must be held "during constant flux." [researched][5] Canon drifts when the white
  space invited player theories the authors then either must canonise (ceding control)
  or contradict (breaking the curtain / forcing a retcon). [synthesis]

**Synthesis for the guide:** the puzzle-hunt/ARG worldbuilding pattern is *implied
depth maintained by per-artefact fidelity plus an inviolable curtain*, traded against
*co-authorship that makes canon a live negotiation*. What fails is not usually a single
plot hole but the cumulative cost of real-time adaptation over a long timeline:
unanticipated player discoveries, dormant artefacts surfacing late, and the temptation
to break the curtain to "correct" the record. The well-run campaign budgets explicit
white space so player-supplied canon is *designed-for* rather than *retconned-around*.
[synthesis]
