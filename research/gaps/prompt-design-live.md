## Prompt Design in Solo Journaling · Live

**Framing.** In solo journaling, a prompt has a recognizable anatomy: a situation
(what is happening), a constraint (what bounds the response), and an effect (what
the act of answering does to the player or the fiction). The gap question is whether
live games — larp, freeform, megagame, murder-mystery — have a documented craft for
their prompt-like devices (scene cards, character secrets, "bangs", round briefings,
sealed-envelope triggers), and how those devices provoke action while preserving
agency. The answer is yes: a mature, if scattered, craft exists, and it converges
on the same situation/constraint/effect skeleton even though the traditions rarely
talk to each other. [synthesis]

### The "bang" is the canonical live prompt

The most explicit prompt-design craft in tabletop/freeform roleplay is the **bang**,
originating in Ron Edwards' *Sorcerer*. A bang is "an event or circumstance that the
character must respond to, and the choice she makes should matter in some interesting
and emotionally compelling way" — the moments when characters "realize they have a
problem right now and have to get moving to deal with it." [researched][2] The
designer's job is to "present and drive Bangs, in the sense of driving a nail or
driving something home," as "a means for moving from decision to decision on the part
of the players." [researched][2]

The agency-preserving rule is explicit and directly parallels journaling-prompt craft:
**a bang presents a situation, never a predetermined outcome.** The Alexandrian's
prep guide frames good vs. bad bangs as a contrast of openings against resolutions —
"Hearing the wolf howl" not "Killing the wolf"; "Discovering the princess is trapped"
not "Saving the princess"; "Hearing police sirens pull up" not "Escaping the police."
A bang "should never dictate a character's response," and "the best bangs are the ones
that provoke the most choices." [researched][3] This is the situation-without-effect
discipline: the prompt supplies the pressure; the player supplies the verb. [synthesis]

A second craft rule is **brevity as an agency safeguard**: "If you're writing more
than a sentence or two about your cool ideas for bangs, you're probably investing too
much in them." The recommendation is to prep "bullets" — half-sketched hooks
customized live to the actual character's circumstances — rather than fully-formed
scripted moments, because over-investment in a prompt's content tempts the author to
also script its resolution. [researched][3] In journaling terms: keep the constraint
tight and the effect-space open.

### Larp / freeform: tent-pole moments and the "seeded opportunity" prompt

Nordic moment-based design works the same skeleton at scene scale. Designers build
**tent-pole moments** — fixed scenes that will happen regardless — but deliberately
leave timing and approach to the players, aiming for scenes players later retell as
"war stories." [researched][1] The agency mechanism is notable for being *covert*:
rather than restricting players with rules, designers seed environmental and
psychological pressure so that "we've almost certainly shut down the player's
desire... but, crucially, they think it's their own choice." [researched][1] In a
documented "Jump" scene, a physical barrier, an armed character, and pre-built dread
together steer non-interference while leaving players feeling they *chose* it. This is
a prompt whose *constraint* is environmental rather than textual — the live medium's
distinctive affordance, with no clean analogue in solo journaling. [synthesis]

A complementary technique is the **seeded-opportunity prompt**: instead of scripting
a moment, the designer introduces a rule or system that makes emergent moments fertile
— e.g. a keyword-prefixed "pulp language" system that produced spontaneous comedic
scenes nobody wrote. [researched][1] River-rafting design extends this to pacing:
open with "the narrow river and the first rapid" — a high-intensity, structured start
that "encourage[s] players to take action early because their boat is already moving."
[researched][1] The prompt here is not a card but a slope: the situation has built-in
momentum so the first action is nearly free.

### Murder-mystery: the structured secret-and-objective prompt

Scripted murder-mystery parties give the most templated, reproducible prompt anatomy,
because amateurs must run them from the page. A character booklet is divided into 4–6
rounds; "within each round are 3 to 4 bullet points of information they need to look
out for or say," giving each player "just enough information to know what they should
do or say, but they have the freedom to act how they would like." [researched][4] The
load-bearing components are written as distinct prompt types:

- **Secrets** — "a guilty secret they want no one else to know," ideally "incriminating
  but doesn't reveal the murderer's identity," so the case resolves by deduction not
  by a single reveal. [researched][4]
- **Objectives/goals** — clarifying the character's drives ("at least four goals being
  a good number"), adding nothing not already in the background. [researched][4]
- **Clues** — "a piece of information that the character knows, which relates to one of
  the plots," seeded across characters so key information circulates, leavened with
  red herrings. [researched][4]
- **Inter-character ties** — "secrets between other characters (blackmail, hatred,
  affairs)" so "as the night goes on there can be action and surprises." [researched][4]

The agency design is *distributed*: no single prompt drives one player to one action;
instead, overlapping secrets and asymmetric clues make every player simultaneously a
prompt-source for others. The constraint ("3–4 bullets per round") paces disclosure;
the effect-space ("act how they would like") stays open. [synthesis]

### Megagame: the briefing as a three-tier prompt stack

Megagames formalize prompts as **briefings**, layered at three scopes: setting
briefings ("make sure all your players are starting on the same page"), team briefings
(public or private by sensitivity), and individual briefings — "a short... backstory,
two or three personal objectives, any stats..., and any significant relationships."
[researched][5] Objectives are designed for productive friction: they "encourage
players on the same team to pull in different directions and have different priorities,
which increases the tensions within the game." [researched][5]

Two megagame-specific prompt-design lessons stand out. First, **disclosure governance**:
secret information ships with Control guidance on whether and when it can surface,
flagging where early revelation would be "game-changing catastrophic." [researched][5]
This is the sealed-envelope/trigger discipline — a prompt with an explicit firing
condition and a designated adjudicator, something solo journaling has no need for.
Second, **agency lives in the gaps, not the rules**: "many of the interesting
interactions within games arise from briefings rather than rules," and creative player
injects are handled by "on the spot adjudication about possibility, plausibility,
probability, resource costs, and... consequences" rather than by pre-written outcomes.
[researched][6] Control's job is to keep the experience "as open ended and real-world
as possible." [researched][6] As with bangs, the prompt frames a situation and the
human facilitator absorbs the open-ended response — the live medium substitutes a
person for the journaling player's own imagination. [synthesis]

### Cross-media synthesis

The four live traditions independently reproduce the solo-journaling prompt skeleton:

- **Situation** — the bang, the tent-pole moment, the round's secret, the briefing's
  objective. Always a pressure, never a resolution. [synthesis]
- **Constraint** — bounded by brevity (bang: "a sentence or two"; mystery: "3–4
  bullets"), by environment (larp's covert barriers), or by disclosure rules
  (megagame firing conditions). [synthesis]
- **Effect** — deliberately left to the player; the shared design failure mode named
  across sources is *dictating the response*. [synthesis][2][3]

The one structural element live prompts add over solo journaling is an **adjudicator
or environment** that absorbs open-ended responses in real time — the larp space,
the Control team, the other players holding interlocking secrets. Where the journaling
player resolves their own prompt internally, live prompts externalize resolution, and
much of the documented craft is about engineering that external surface (covert
constraints, distributed clues, on-the-spot adjudication) so the prompt provokes
without prescribing. [synthesis]
