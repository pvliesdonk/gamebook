## Team Sizes & the Bottleneck Problem · Tabletop

The tabletop sweet spot is consistently cited as roughly four to six players for D&D 5e and comparable
systems, balancing role coverage, tactical depth, and pacing [researched][1]. The constraint is not
mere preference: the *same* party size that adds tactical richness also creates dead air. With five or
six players, "a couple of players will be doing nothing for significant periods," whereas three to four
players gives everyone strong roleplay share [researched][2]. The trade-off is explicit — go larger only
if some players are content to cede the lead [researched][2].

The bottleneck-player equivalent at the table is the **spotlight hog**, and the design literature treats it
as a structural problem, not just a misbehaving-player problem. The Alexandrian's rule is to "split spotlight
time *by player, not group*" and to watch for the "lone wolf spotlight" — one PC wandering off solo who ends
up consuming half the GM's attention, structurally rewarding disruptive play [researched][3]. The designed-around
countermeasure is encounter and scenario structure that lets multiple players act simultaneously or showcases
distinct character niches, so spotlight is distributed by the *scenario's shape* rather than by who talks
loudest [researched][1][4]. The silent-player mirror image: spotlight is "the opportunity for players to
explore and demonstrate their character," so a player who never receives it is being structurally denied the
core experience — addressed by direct prompts ("what does your character do?") and niche-protected design,
not left to chance [researched][4][5]. [synthesis] This makes party size a *designed constraint* with the
same shape as the digital bottleneck problem: capacity is bounded, and the design's job is to ration a scarce
attention resource fairly rather than let it pool around the loudest or most aggressive node.

## Spectator-Friendly Design · Tabletop

Actual Play (livestreamed/recorded sessions) has pushed tabletop design toward an explicit *dual audience* —
the players at the table and the viewers watching — with "rules that reward cinematic action" and campaign
structures that "cater to both the players and the fans" [researched][6]. Viewing is generative, not passive:
good chemistry on screen "makes viewers imagine what they might be able to do in their own games," and many
shows ship explicit "how-to" onboarding content for would-be GMs and players [researched][6]. Audiences are
treated as "active participants… reacting in chat, influencing story beats" in real time [researched][6].

At the physical table, the relevant design unit is the **audience stance**: the "Audience/Observer" player
"engages by watching and should not be forced into the spotlight," and players who feel their contributions
matter "stay more attentive when not directly involved" by taking notes or watching closely [researched][5].
The hard limit is real — "being expected to serve as an audience for hours on end is not the path to a fun
interactive experience" [researched][5]. [synthesis] Three distinct non-playing observers therefore each want
a different design accommodation: the *watching partner/spectator* wants legibility (visible state, audible
narration); the *learning newcomer* wants a low-stakes on-ramp (how-to scaffolding, an easy first character);
the *streamed audience* wants cinematic legibility plus interaction hooks. Designing for the observer is the
inverse of the bottleneck problem — instead of rationing scarce spotlight, you are making the *un-spotlit*
state engaging.

## One-on-One Scene Design · Tabletop

The split/solo scene is treated as a craft skill with named failure modes. Splitting the party is "Easy Mode
for effective pacing" because the GM gains interior cuts — cutting mid-scene for cliffhangers rather than only
at scene boundaries [researched][3]. Personal scenes are the canonical use case: "it's hard to woo your lady
love with four wingmen hovering over your shoulder," and solo sessions let a PC make status-appropriate
connections and let the GM foreshadow [researched][3].

The **waiting-table problem** is the central constraint and has an explicit technique: cut *into* scenes,
not between them — "it's almost always better to cut too often than it is to wait too long," switching away
whenever the active group is merely deliberating or rolling, so waiting players still get bonus play time
[researched][3]. Time need not be synced between groups: cut "forward" to a traveling party's arrival, then
"backward" to a stationary one, without causality violation [researched][3]. Simultaneous combats run off a
single shared initiative list with cuts driven by initiative order — "training wheels" for the technique
[researched][3]. [synthesis] Consent/buy-in is the under-documented edge here: the Alexandrian source implies
buy-in ("read the room," players "enjoy audience stance") but does not make it explicit [researched][3]. The
genuine design gap is that a *fully private* one-on-one scene (player removed from the table) carries a
consent and trust load — secrets withheld from other players, asymmetric information — that the published
craft literature largely leaves to GM judgment rather than mechanizing.

## Accessibility in Live & Interactive Games · Tabletop

Meeple Like Us is the canonical tabletop-accessibility tradition, evaluating "physical, cognitive, visual,
emotional and sociological accessibility" via per-game teardowns that apply a fixed set of lenses
[researched][7]. The formalized version — the **Meeple Centred Design** heuristic toolkit — defines seven
interconnected categories: *visual* (colour blindness, contrast, font legibility, token tactility, depth
judgment, dice readability), *cognitive* (literacy demand, game-state complexity, memory load, turn-flow
consistency, multitasking), *emotional* (despair mechanics, player elimination, "take that," ganging-up),
*physical* (card/token size and weight, manipulation frequency, board density), *communication* (reading
complexity, auditory cues and alternatives, mandatory sound), *socioeconomic* (artwork inclusivity, cost per
player, player-count efficiency), and *intersectional* (how multiple impairments compound — hand size, hidden
info, downtime) [researched][8].

A load-bearing heuristic: a game should "never rely on colour as the only channel of information" — good
practice pairs every colour with a distinct symbol, or uses component *size* hierarchies (secondary larger
than primary, tertiary larger than secondary) as a redundant channel [researched][7]. The toolkit's framing
principle is that "all accessibility in tabletop gaming is situational" — context-dependent assessment over
universal prescription [researched][8]. [synthesis] The design-layer takeaway: accessibility here is a
*review lens applied to physical artifacts*, the tabletop analogue of a digital a11y audit, and it surfaces
modalities (component weight, tactile differentiation, despair/elimination as an *emotional*-accessibility
concern) that have no clean screen-based equivalent.

## Mixed-Ability Groups & Universal Design · Tabletop

The serving-one-table problem is distinct from per-disability accommodation: a single mixed-ability or
multi-generational group must be served simultaneously. Universal design answers by "embedding universal
design principles from the start, rather than treating accessibility as an afterthought," which yields games
"not only essential for players with disabilities but… more enjoyable for everyone" [researched][9]. Concrete
mixed-age heuristics: lean on **redundant skill channels** like pattern recognition that is "accessible to
younger players whilst maintaining adult challenge," and cap teach time — "rules that can be explained in
under five minutes," because pausing to look up clarifications "already loses the younger audience"
[researched][10].

[synthesis] The mechanism that unifies this cell with the colour-channel heuristic above is **redundant input
modes**: where single-player accessibility removes a barrier for one person, mixed-ability universal design
provides *multiple simultaneous on-ramps* (visual + tactile + verbal; pattern-matching + arithmetic) so that
players of different abilities engage the same game state through different channels at one table.
Reading-load distribution — not every player must parse the rulebook — is the social-layer analogue of the
component-redundancy heuristic: the group, not the individual, is the unit of accessibility.

## Designer-Community Trust in Long-Form Hunts · Tabletop

The multi-year designer↔community trust compact has a clear tabletop analogue in **organized / living-campaign
play**. The living-campaign model is old: the RPGA launched its first living campaign in 1987, where players
across many locations "could impact the storyline via their reported actions," and Wizards frames the
Adventurers League (est. 2014 with 5e) as the "legacy ongoing campaign" [researched][11]. The trust mechanics
are concrete: a published **errata** policy ("apply errata to characters; if that makes a character illegal,
make the minimal changes to bring it legal"), ongoing official FAQ documents, and a tiered authority structure
in which "Premier Organizers" are granted official slices of the campaign setting to author sanctioned content
[researched][11]. As of April 2026 WotC was expanding organized-play formats (Legends of Greyhawk, return of
D&D Encounters), signaling continued investment in the compact [researched][11].

[synthesis] The structural match to the long-form-hunt trust compact: a *persistent shared canon* the
community contributes to over years, a *legibility guarantee* (errata + designer FAQ as the equivalent of
puzzle-hunt designer Q&A and post-hoc rulings), and *delegated trust* (Premier Organizers ≈ trusted community
authors). The difference worth flagging: organized-play trust is mediated by a *publisher* with formal
sanctioning power, whereas hunt-community trust is often a flatter designer-to-solver relationship — so the
tabletop analogue is the *institutionally-mediated* end of the trust spectrum, not the pure peer compact.
